## What This Is

A reference list of robotic AI phrasing and structural tics to kill on sight. Use alongside your writing to catch and eliminate AI-sounding language before publishing.

## The Antipatterns

### 1. AI Intensifiers
**Words:** "delve into," "unpack," "leverage," "empower," "unlock," "transformative," "paradigm shift," "cutting-edge," "synergy"

❌ "Let's leverage this opportunity to unlock new paradigm shifts."  
✓ "Here's how we capitalize on this."

These words scream automation. Use concrete verbs (build, test, measure, ship) instead.

### 2. AI Connectors
**Words:** "Moreover," "Furthermore," "In addition to," "As previously mentioned," "It's worth noting that"

❌ "Moreover, this finding adds another dimension to the analysis."  
✓ "This changes how we think about capacity."

Formal connectors stall momentum. Use simple coordinating words or start fresh sentences.

### 3. AI Adjectives
**Words:** "comprehensive," "extensive," "insightful," "sophisticated," "nuanced," "multifaceted"

❌ "This comprehensive analysis reveals nuanced dynamics."  
✓ "This analysis shows three shifts."

Name what you found instead of describing how thorough you were.

### 4. AI Conclusions
**Words:** "In summary," "To conclude," "As discussed," "It is clear that," "The evidence suggests"

❌ "In summary, the data indicates that Premium has adoption challenges."  
✓ "37% of Premium customers don't use the features they paid for."

State the fact. Skip the disclaimer language.

### 5. "It's Not X, It's Y" Formula
❌ "It's not a pricing problem — it's a packaging problem."  
✓ "The price is right. Customers don't understand what they're paying for."

The negation adds words and hedges. State the positive claim directly.

### 6. Em Dash Overuse (—)
AI scatters em dashes everywhere — in every paragraph — sometimes twice — like commas don't exist.

❌ "Premium is the gravity center — 56% of MRR — and faces three activation challenges — feature engagement gaps, pricing perception, and lack of clear value definition."

✓ "Premium is 56% of MRR but faces three activation challenges: feature engagement gaps, pricing perception, and lack of value clarity."

Use colons for lists, periods for breaks, commas for asides. Not em dashes.

### 7. "Let's Break This Down" Move
❌ "Let's break this down. There are three ways to interpret this."  
✓ "Three ways to interpret this:"

Kill the filler. Go straight to the substance.

### 8. Dramatic One-Liner Paragraph
❌ Single-sentence paragraph.  
❌ Followed by explanation.

This looks like a TED talk slide. Just write it out:  
✓ "Premium is 56% of MRR but 37% of tenants have zero engagement with gated features."

### 9. "Not Just X, But Y" / "Not Only X, But Also Y"
❌ "This is not just a product gap — it's a strategic risk."  
✓ "This is a strategic risk."

The double structure adds heft without adding meaning. Be direct.

### 10. Colon-Into-List Setup
❌ "There are three things to consider here:"  
✓ "Three activation levers:"

Skip the preamble. Name what you're listing and list it.

### 11. Parenthetical Percentage Aside
❌ "Most customers (78%) never expand beyond IT."  
✓ "78% of customers never expand beyond IT."

Parentheses bury numbers. Let them stand.

### 12. Semicolons as List Separators
❌ "The gaps include CMDB maturity; reporting depth; change management workflows; and identity architecture."  
✓ "The gaps:
- CMDB maturity
- Reporting depth
- Change management workflows
- Identity architecture"

Semicolons are for equal clauses, not list items. Use bullets or line breaks.

### 13. Certainly/Absolutely Openers
❌ "Certainly! I'd be happy to help with that."  
✓ "Here's how to approach this."

No filler. No false cheer. Get to work.

## Why It Matters for PMs

**You are trying to persuade, decide, and move.** Every wasted word is thinking that doesn't happen. Robotic language makes readers distrust you — it signals you're copying a template, not thinking.

When you write like yourself (direct, concrete, specific), readers feel you've done the work. That matters for:
- Selling your strategy to leadership
- Onboarding new team members  
- Building credibility as a decision-maker

The best PM writing feels like you're talking to a trusted colleague — no corporate armor, no filler. That comes from killing antipatterns relentlessly.
