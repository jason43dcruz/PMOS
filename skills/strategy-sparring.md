# Skill: Strategy Sparring

When Jason asks you to spar on a strategy, evaluate a recommendation, or pressure-test a doc — use these moves. They're ordered by how hard they push. Start gentle, escalate as needed.

## Before sparring — prep step

Before the first question, read the relevant domain context:
1. **Always read:** `Knowledge/knowledge-refs.md` — find the docs relevant to the strategy topic (pricing, competitive, GTM, etc.)
2. **Always read:** `Knowledge/lennys-greatest-hits.md` — synthesised PM frameworks from 60 Lenny's Podcast episodes. Covers strategy, positioning, growth, leadership, influence, and AI product thinking. Use this to pressure-test with external best-practice, not just internal data. Raw transcripts in `Knowledge/lennys-podcast-transcripts/` if you need a specific verbatim quote.
3. **For pricing/packaging:** Read the P&P guidelines, WAC page, and Eleanor's LRP model.
4. **For competitive positioning:** Read the competitive synthesis page, Gartner/Forrester analysis, and the competitor gating table from supporting data.
5. **For product strategy:** Read the full edition strategy doc (parent + child pages), Anand's capability map, and any relevant Secoda docs.
6. **For GTM/motions:** Read the upgrade signal analysis, channel partner docs, and any relevant S360 data.

The goal is to spar with the company's actual context, not generic frameworks. If you don't have domain expertise, say so — don't fake it.

## Rules

- **One question at a time.** Ask one, get an answer, then move to the next. Don't dump.
- **Be concrete.** Name the specific claim, number, or framing you're challenging. Don't say "this could be stronger" — say exactly what's wrong and why.
- **Listen to lived experience.** If Jason tells you something contradicts the data or your framework, dig into *why* before defaulting to "the data says otherwise." His signal from customers, sales, and internal conversations is data too.
- **Never present assumptions as facts.** If you're working from general knowledge rather than internal data, say so.
- **Check your own work.** If you helped build the strategy, you have self-bias. Actively look for where your own framing might be wrong.
- **Let the data make the argument.** Don't say "you don't have PMF" — show the 4 data points and let Jason connect the dots. Stating the conclusion explicitly is weaker than showing the evidence.

## The Moves

### 1. Internal contradiction
Find two claims in the doc that point in opposite directions.
> *"Standard caps push customers up to Premium. Enterprise-first pushes HT customers down to Premium. So Premium is simultaneously the upgrade destination AND the downsell landing zone. What's the distinct customer identity?"*

### 2. Alternate conclusion
Same data, different answer. Force the question of why this interpretation is right and the other isn't.
> *"Someone could read this and say: just collapse to two tiers. Why is Premium the right bet vs. collapsing?"*

### 3. Data audit
For every claim on the page, ask: is there sufficient evidence? Be specific about which claims are solid and which are thin.
> *"The 3–6× value claim has no source cited. The 60–80% range overstates what the signal data shows. The automation cap claim is qualitative, not quantified."*

### 4. Source verification
Don't trust numbers you helped create. Go back to the source and check.
> *"Where did the 77% come from? Let me re-read the supporting data page... it says Prem→Ent upgrades, but DC migration isn't a Prem→Ent upgrade. The number is being applied to the wrong cohort."*

### 5. Unwritten assumption
Surface the thing everyone in the room is thinking but nobody has written down.
> *"You just said Premium doesn't have PMF. That's the most important sentence in the whole strategy and it's not on the page."*

### 6. Misapplied evidence
Check whether a data point is being used in the right context.
> *"The automation cap finding is cited as a Standard→Premium upgrade trigger, but the source says it's an Enterprise purchase trigger. That's a different motion."*

### 7. Adjacent implications
Read external docs, decisions, or timelines and ask: does this change the strategy?
> *"UBP enforcement is Oct '26. Your Premium rocks target Q2 FY27. Those timelines collide — what happens if customers hit caps before Premium is ready?"*

### 8. Sequencing test
Ask what has to be true *first*, and in what order.
> *"You can't fix the motion until the product earns it. What are the 3 things that have to be true before the edition strategy can work?"*

### 9. Dangerous misread
Ask what the audience could walk away believing that would be wrong.
> *"What's the one thing the PSR audience could misunderstand that would be dangerous?"*

### 10. Constraint or choice?
Is this a decision you're making, or a constraint you're operating within? Name it.
> *"The four-tier structure is a company constraint, not a choice. Say that — it removes a false debate."*

## When to stop

Stop sparring when:
- Every claim has evidence behind it (or is explicitly flagged as directional)
- Internal contradictions have been resolved or acknowledged
- The sequencing is explicit — what comes first, what depends on what
- Jason says "I'm happy with this" or pivots to execution

## After sparring

- Offer to rewrite or update the page with what was surfaced
- Track any open data gaps (numbers to pull, analyses to rerun) as backlog items
- Note the sparring session in session-log.md so moves that landed can be refined over time
