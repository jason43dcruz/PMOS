# Skill: Write Like Me

**Trigger:** Any writing task — "Draft…," "Write…," "Summarize…," when producing text for the user

When the user asks you to write anything for them, apply this skill. Every draft must sound like them, not like a chatbot.

---

## Step 1: Read Your Sources

Before writing, read:
- **Knowledge/writing-style.md** — Voice, tone, phrases you use, words you never use
- **Knowledge/product-context.md** — Audience priorities (when drafting for someone specific)
- **think-like-me.md** — Your reasoning style (for strategy docs, decisions, recommendations)

---

## Step 2: Voice & Structure

- **"We" by default, "you" when explaining.** Use first person plural for team/product direction. Switch to second person when walking through a setup or flow.
- **TLDR first.** Strategy docs open with a bold **TLDR:** — 2–3 sentences that give the full picture. Don't make people dig.
- **Lead with the problem, then earn the solution.** Don't bury the answer. State what's broken, then how to fix it.
- **Short paragraphs.** 1–3 sentences. Break after each distinct thought.
- **Specific over vague.** Use concrete examples, numbers, dollar figures, data links, and customer quotes. Name the companies. Name the roles.
- **Matter-of-fact tone.** Don't hedge. Say what's happening, why it matters, and what to do about it. Use "We believe..." for strategic convictions.
- **Brutally honest.** Name the deals we lost and why. Include "Why we lose today" sections in strategy docs. Don't sugarcoat competitive gaps.
- **Casual asides are welcome.** A quick parenthetical or self-deprecating note keeps things human — "tread carefully. PM mocks ahead" energy.
- **Trust the reader.** Don't over-explain obvious implications. If they're reading your doc, they have context.
- **British spelling** where it comes naturally (optimise, prioritise, customise).
- **Calibrate by audience.** Direct with managers. Collaborative with peers. Confident with leadership. Adjust per Knowledge/product-context.md.

## Step 3: Avoid AI-Tell Words & Patterns

These words and patterns make writing sound AI-generated. **Do not use them.** Based on [Wikipedia: Signs of AI writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing).

### Overused AI vocabulary (avoid)
- **Verbs:** leverage, utilize, streamline, harness, foster, cultivate, facilitate, optimize, enhance, empower, unlock, tap into, dive into, delve, delve into, navigate, revolutionize, transform, drive (as in "drive results"), showcase, highlight (as verb), underscore (as verb), garner, align with
- **Adjectives:** robust, comprehensive, seamless, cutting-edge, innovative, pioneering, holistic, scalable, best-in-class, impactful, game-changing, disruptive, user-centric, vibrant, crucial, pivotal, intricate/intricacies, enduring, valuable, key (as adjective)
- **Nouns:** synergy, ecosystem, landscape (as abstract noun), journey (when overused), paradigm shift, tapestry (as abstract noun), testament, interplay

### Significance/legacy puffery (avoid)
- stands as, serves as, is a testament/reminder, vital/significant/crucial/pivotal/key role/moment
- underscores/highlights its importance/significance, reflects broader, symbolizing ongoing/enduring
- contributing to the, setting the stage for, marking/shaping the, represents/marks a shift
- key turning point, evolving landscape, focal point, indelible mark, deeply rooted

### Superficial analysis phrases (avoid)
- highlighting/underscoring/emphasizing …, ensuring …, reflecting/symbolizing …, contributing to …
- cultivating/fostering … (figurative), encompassing …, valuable insights, align/resonate with

### Promotional language (avoid)
- boasts a, vibrant, rich (figurative), profound, enhancing, showcasing, exemplifies
- commitment to, natural beauty, nestled, in the heart of, groundbreaking, renowned, featuring, diverse array

### Simple copulatives (prefer)
- Use **"is"** and **"are"** instead of: serves as, stands as, marks, represents, boasts, features, offers
- Example: "Gallery 825 **is** LAAA's exhibition space" not "Gallery 825 **serves as** LAAA's exhibition space"

### Negative parallelisms (avoid)
- "Not just X, but also Y" — sounds formulaic
- "It's not X, it's Y" — overused balance construction
- Prefer direct statements over these constructions

### Rule of three (use sparingly)
- AI overuses "adjective, adjective, adjective" and "phrase, phrase, and phrase"
- Vary structure. Don't default to triples.

### Phrases to avoid
- "at the end of the day", "it's important to note", "in today's world", "when it comes to"
- "in order to" (use "to"), "a wide range of", "plays a crucial role", "moving forward"
- "at this point in time", "when all is said and done", "the fact that" (use "that" or rephrase)
- "Additionally," (especially at sentence start), "In summary", "In conclusion", "Overall"
- "I hope this helps", "Of course!", "Certainly!", "Would you like...", "let me know"

### Vague attributions (avoid)
- "Industry reports", "Observers have cited", "Experts argue", "Some critics argue"
- "several sources/publications" when only few are cited

### Outline-like conclusions (avoid)
- "Despite its … faces several challenges …", "Despite these challenges"
- "Challenges and Legacy", "Future Outlook" as rigid section formulas

### Rhetorical tics (avoid — biggest AI tells)
- **"It's not X, it's Y"** — punchy once, pattern by the third time. Prefer: "Packaging is the real issue, not pricing."
- **"Not just X, but Y"** — same energy, same overuse. Say what matters first.
- **Em dash overuse** — one per paragraph max. Never two in one sentence. Commas and colons exist.
- **"Let's break this down" / "Let me walk you through"** — just present the information.
- **Dramatic one-liner paragraphs** — "And that changes everything." Once per doc, maybe. AI does it every few paragraphs.
- **Bolding every other phrase** — if everything is emphasised, nothing is. Bold one thing per paragraph.
- **"There are three things to consider:"** — just start the list. Don't announce it.
- **"On one hand / on the other hand"** — take a position. Name the risk after.
- **"Great question!" / "Certainly!" / "Absolutely!"** — just do the thing.
- **Recapping at the end** — if they just read it, they don't need a summary. End with the action.

### Structural tells (avoid)
- **"In today's rapidly evolving, increasingly complex..."** — start with a fact or number instead.
- **"This is a multifaceted challenge requiring a nuanced approach..."** — name the actual tradeoffs.
- **Exhaustive bullet lists** — if you have 12 bullets, you have 3 that matter and 9 that don't. Cut the 9.

### The smell test
Read it aloud. If it sounds like a LinkedIn post (buzzwords), consulting deck (frameworks, no specifics), press release (too positive), or textbook (too even-handed) → rewrite like you're explaining it to a smart colleague over coffee.

### One rule
**If a sentence could appear in any company's strategy doc with zero changes, it's not saying anything.** Make it specific to your situation, your data, your customers.

**Add your own banned words to Knowledge/writing-style.md.** Check there first.

## Step 4: Use Your Phrases

- Use phrases from Knowledge/writing-style.md that sound like you. Key ones:
  - "TLDR:" — bold, at the top of strategy docs
  - "There are two issues here:" — then numbered specifics
  - "The current process is that..." — grounding in reality before proposing change
  - "We believe..." — stating strategic convictions without hedging
  - "This suggests a convergence into X plays..." — framing strategic options
  - "Note that..." — for caveats and important context
  - "This does not solve the problem" — when being direct about limitations
  - "We should also expect..." — flagging upcoming risks
  - "We don't expect to replace X but will work alongside..." — positioning against competitors
  - "We win because..." — articulating competitive advantage
  - "From a customer -" — followed by a direct quote, naming the company
  - "This is mostly because..." — plain causal explanation
  - "The ideal solution would be to..." — when proposing direction
  - "See [link] for more detail" — pointing to source material
- When in doubt, write in plain, direct language. No corporate jargon.

## Step 5: Document Structure

Match the structure to the document type. These patterns come from how the user actually writes:

**Major Strategy Docs:**
1. **TLDR** at the very top — bold, 2–3 sentences, full picture
2. "What we believe" — two-column table (Market Shifts | Strategic Implications) with status tags
3. **North star** — bold one-liner for the aspirational end state
4. Strategic opportunities — numbered plays with bold names, resourcing % splits, named competitors
5. Customer voice — direct quotes from real companies organized by theme with status labels
6. "Why we lose today" — honest assessment naming specific lost deals
7. Comparison tables by edition/tier or buyer type
8. Phase-based roadmap with dates, feature lists, status tags (in planning, unfunded, catch up, differentiator)
9. Opportunity sizing with dollar figures and sourced data
10. Success measures per phase
11. FAQ and Appendix with links

**Smaller Vision / Strategy Docs:**
1. Vision statement (bold, 1–2 sentences)
2. Current state — what exists today
3. Problem space — what's broken, with data
4. Strategy — what we believe and why
5. Comparison tables — Current vs. Desired, by edition/tier
6. Focus areas with estimated effort

**DACIs / Decision Docs:**
1. DACI metadata table up top
2. Background — plain description
3. Current state
4. Data for decision support — tables, numbers, sourced links
5. Options table — one column per option, rows for description, pros/cons, risks, cost
6. Actions — checkboxes assigned to people
7. Outcome — short, clear

**Problem / Feature Docs:**
1. Problem statement (bold)
2. Evidence it's a problem — data, tickets, customer quotes
3. User stories table with priority labels (must have / should have)
4. Sample use cases from real customers
5. Short term vs. Long term framing

**General formatting habits:**
- **Bold** key terms when introducing a concept or contrasting options
- Parenthetical asides for quick context
- Numbered lists for sequential steps, bullets for non-sequential
- Numbered strategic plays: "This suggests a convergence into 2 plays..." then bold-named options
- Name mental models explicitly: "We can broadly slice the problem into 3 areas..."
- Tables for any comparison — always. Options, editions, features, buyer types
- Embed customer quotes with company name and role: "From a customer -"
- Link to source data (analytics, support tickets, spreadsheets, analyst reports, Salesforce)
- Status labels in tables: must have, should have, new, catch up, differentiator, in planning, unfunded
- Resourcing percentages alongside priorities (e.g., "50% resourcing", "10% resourcing")
- Specific dollar figures for opportunity sizing with linked sources
- Emoji only as section header markers (🏆 How we win, 🚀 How we execute) — not in prose
- "WIP" at the top of unfinished docs

## Step 6: Style Pitfalls

- **Em dashes:** Use them — but not three per sentence. Commas and parentheses work too.
- **Boldface:** Use for key terms and emphasis inline. This matches the user's style. But don't bold entire sentences.
- **Emoji:** Only in status labels or table markers. Not in running prose.

---

## Step 7: Before Delivering

1. **Scan for banned words.** Replace any AI-tell words with simpler alternatives.
2. **Check sentence variety.** AI tends toward similar sentence lengths. Vary short and long.
3. **Prefer "is/are".** Replace "serves as", "stands as", "features" with simpler copulatives.
4. **Add a human touch.** A customer quote, a specific number, a parenthetical aside, or a direct opinion.
5. **Check the structure.** Does the doc match the patterns in Step 5? Problem before solution? Tables for comparisons?
6. **Read it aloud.** If it sounds like a press release, rewrite it. If it sounds like something you'd say in a meeting, ship it.

## Step 8: Publishing Honesty Rules

Apply these whenever drafting anything that will be shared — Slack posts, LinkedIn, Confluence, stakeholder comms.

- **Don't revert Jason's edits.** When he softens language in a draft, that's deliberate. Read his version before redrafting anything he's already touched. Never restore stronger language he's consciously tempered.
- **"may never" not "never".** Don't overclaim certainty. "We may never have tested this manually" is honest. "We never would have found this" is not — we ran a systematic analysis, not an exhaustive one.
- **Statistical claims need caveats.** If a stat comes from the discovery cohort (not a holdout), say so. "In our cohort" not "of all users." Don't present in-sample stats as out-of-sample predictions.
- **Posts are teasers, not substitutes.** Slack and LinkedIn posts drive to the doc. The analysis, methodology, and caveats live in Confluence. The post makes someone want to click — it doesn't give them everything.
- **No jargon in public-facing content.** Internal acronyms (PQL, HT/LT, CEE, ServCo) mean nothing outside Atlassian. Spell out or replace before anything goes external.
- **Atlassian-safe by default.** No internal financials, customer names, signal names, lift scores, or confidential strategy details in LinkedIn or external posts. Specifics stay in Confluence.

## When in Doubt

Ask: *Would I say this in a Slack message to a colleague?* If not, simplify.
