# Writing Style Guide

<!-- Derived from analysis of Confluence pages: vision docs, DACIs, strategy pieces, deep dives, and competitive analyses. -->

## Voice & Tone

- First person plural ("we") when talking about the team or product direction. Second person ("you") when walking someone through a flow or setup.
- Direct and matter-of-fact. State what's happening, why it matters, what to do. Don't hedge.
- Short paragraphs — 1–3 sentences. Break after each distinct thought.
- Lead with the problem, then earn the solution. Don't bury the point.
- Open strategy docs with a bold **TLDR:** that gives the reader the full picture in 2–3 sentences.
- Use "We believe..." to state strategic convictions directly. No weasel words.
- Brutally honest about weaknesses — name the deals we lost and why. "Why we lose today" energy.
- Casual asides are fine — "tread carefully. PM mocks ahead" energy.
- Trust the reader. Don't over-explain obvious implications.
- British spelling where it comes naturally (optimise, prioritise, customise).
- Comfortable saying "WIP" or "TBD" — no need to pretend everything is polished.

## Phrases I Use

- "TLDR:" (bold, at the top of strategy docs)
- "There are two issues here:" (then numbered specifics)
- "The current process is that..."
- "We propose to solve the following pain points"
- "We believe..." (for stating strategic convictions)
- "This suggests a convergence into X plays..."
- "This is mostly because..."
- "Note that..." (for caveats and important context)
- "We should also expect..." (forward-looking risks)
- "This does not solve the problem"
- "The ideal solution would be to..."
- "We don't expect to replace X but will work alongside..."
- "Early thinking is captured at..."
- "From a customer -" (followed by a direct quote)
- "Thus we have identified the need to..."
- "This represents an opportunity for..."
- "In terms of how..."
- "We win because..." (when articulating competitive advantage)
- "Why we lose today" (followed by honest examples with named companies)
- "See [link] for more detail"

## Words I Never Use

- leverage, utilize, streamline, harness, foster, empower
- robust, comprehensive, seamless, cutting-edge, innovative, holistic
- synergy, paradigm shift, ecosystem (as abstract noun)
- deep dive (as a verb — fine as a noun in page titles)
- "plays a crucial role", "at the end of the day"
- "it's important to note" — just say "Note that..."
- "stakeholders" — name the actual people or teams
- "align" as a verb (say "matches" or "fits with")

## Structure Patterns

### Vision / Strategy Docs
1. **TLDR** at the very top — bold, 2–3 sentences covering the full picture
2. "What we believe" — two-column table (Market Shifts | Strategic Implications) with status tags (NEW + EXTERNAL, EXISTING, etc.)
3. **North star** — bold one-liner capturing the aspirational end state
4. Strategic opportunities — numbered plays with bold names, resourcing percentages (e.g., "50% resourcing"), and named competitors
5. Customer voice / Customer needs — direct quotes from real companies, organized by theme, with status labels (resolve, detect, prevent)
6. "Why we lose today" — honest assessment naming specific deals lost and why
7. Comparison tables — Current vs. Desired, broken out by edition/tier or buyer type
8. Phase-based roadmap with specific dates, feature lists, and status tags (in planning, unfunded, NEW, catch up, differentiator)
9. Opportunity sizing with specific dollar figures and sourced data
10. Success measures per phase
11. FAQ and Appendix sections with links

### Smaller Vision / Strategy Docs
1. Vision statement (bold, 1–2 sentences)
2. Current state — what exists today, with specifics
3. Problem space — what's broken or missing, with data
4. Strategy — what we believe and why
5. Comparison tables — Current vs. Desired, broken out by edition/tier
6. Focus areas with estimated effort (dev weeks)

### DACIs / Decision Docs
1. DACI metadata table (Status, Impact, Driver, Approver, Contributors, Informed, Due date, Outcome)
2. Background — plain description of the situation
3. Current state — what's happening now
4. Data for decision support — tables and numbers, sourced with links
5. Options table — columns per option, rows for description, pros/cons, risks, estimated cost
6. Actions — checkboxes assigned to specific people
7. Outcome — short, clear statement of what was decided

### Problem / Feature Docs
1. Problem statement (bold, 1–2 sentences)
2. "How do we know this is a problem?" — link to data, JAC tickets, customer quotes
3. User stories table (User Story | Comments columns, with priority labels like "must have" / "should have")
4. Sample use cases with real customer examples
5. Short term vs. Long term framing

### General Habits
- Heavy use of **bold** for key terms inline — especially when introducing a concept or contrasting options
- Parenthetical asides for quick context: "(not including Halp)", "(This is the current capability used for abuse)"
- Numbered lists for sequential steps, bullet lists for non-sequential items
- Numbered strategic plays: "This suggests a convergence into 2 plays..." then bold-named options
- Embed direct customer quotes with attribution — name the company, name the role
- Name mental models explicitly: "We can broadly slice the problem into 3 areas..."
- Link to source data (Splunk, Redash, Google Sheets, JAC tickets, analyst reports, Salesforce)
- Tables for any comparison — options, editions, features, use cases, buyer types
- Status labels in tables: must have, should have, new, completed, catch up, differentiator, in planning, unfunded
- Resourcing percentages alongside strategic priorities (e.g., "50% resourcing", "10% resourcing")
- Specific dollar figures for opportunity sizing with linked sources
- Emoji sparingly — only as section header markers (🏆 How we win, 🚀 How we execute, 🌏 Where we play)
- "WIP" at the top of unfinished docs — no pretense
